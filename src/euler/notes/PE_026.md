# [PE_026](../PE_026.java)

First we prove that for  any $n$, the reciprocal decimal cycle length is equal to the multiplicative order of $10$ modulo $n_0$, where $n = 2^a \cdot 5^b \cdot n_0$, $\text{gcd}(10, n_0) = 1$.

## Proof

Lemma 1: for any $n$ with $\text{gcd}(10, n) = 1$, $\text{cycleLength}(n)$ is equal to the multiplicative order of $10$ modulo $n$.  
Proof:   
Any $i$-th remainder in the long division of $\frac{1}{n}$ is $r_i \equiv 10^i \pmod{n}$.  
The decimal repeats with period $T$ starting at position $i$ iff
$r_i \equiv r_{i+T} \pmod{n} \Leftrightarrow 10^i \equiv 10^{i+T} \pmod{n}$.  
Since $\text{gcd}(10, n) = 1$, we can divide by $10^i$, giving $10^T \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$, meaning the minimal solution for $T$ is $\text{ord}_{n}(10)$ ◻

Lemma 2: for any $n$, $\text{cycleLength}(n) = \text{cycleLength}(n_0)$, where $n = 2^a \cdot 5^b \cdot n_0$, $a \ge 0$, $b \ge 0$, $\text{gcd}(10, n_0) = 1$.  
Proof: let $s = \text{max}(a, b)$ and $C = 2^{s-a} \cdot 5^{s-b}$. Then $\frac{1}{n} = \frac{C}{n_0 \cdot 10^s}$. Since the decimal of $\frac{C}{n_0}$ is just the decimal of $\frac{1}{n}$ shifted by $s$ places, $\text{cycleLength}(n) = \text{cycleLength}(\frac{n_0}{C})$ ◻

Any $i$-th remainder in the long division of $\frac{C}{n_0}$ is $r_i \equiv C \cdot 10^i \pmod{n_0}$.  
The decimal repeats with period $T$ iff $C \cdot 10^i \equiv C \cdot 10^{i+T} \pmod{n_0}$.   
Since $\text{gcd}(C, n_0) = 1$ and $\text{gcd}(10, n_0) = 1$, we can divide by $C \cdot 10^i$ to get $10^T \equiv 1 \pmod{n_0}$. By the same argument as Lemma 1, $T = \text{ord}_{n_0}(10)$.

By Lemma 1 and Lemma 2, $\text{cycleLength}(n) = \text{ord}_{n_0}(10)$, where $n = 2^a \cdot 5^b \cdot n_0$ and $\text{gcd}(10, n_0) = 1$ ∎

## Algorithm

Given limit $L$, starting from $i = L-1$ and iterating downwards, we can skip multiples of $2$ and $5$, since any $\text{cycleLength}(i) = \text{cycleLength}(2i) = \text{cycleLength}(5i)$, and we will check the base $i$ later.  
Also, when $i$ becomes lower than the longest found cycle length, we can stop the search, as $\text{cycleLength}(i) \le i-1$ as per the [pigeonhole principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pigeonhole_principle).

We can also optimise finding $\text{ord}_n(10)$. [Euler's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_theorem) asserts:  
$a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$.  
Since $\text{ord}_n(10)$ is the smallest positive integer solution $T$ for $10^T \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$, we can write $\phi(n) = q \cdot d + r$, $0 \le r < d$. Then:  
$1 \equiv 10^{\phi(n)} \equiv 10^{qd + r} \equiv (10^d)^q \cdot 10^r \equiv 1^q \cdot 10^r \equiv 10^r \pmod{n}$.  
So $r = \log_{10}1 = 0$, meaning $d$ is a divisor of $\phi(n)$.  
Than means we can compute $\phi(n)$ find its divisors, and check each divisor $d$ for $10^d \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$.
