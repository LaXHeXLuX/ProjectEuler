## Trivial examples
When trying to generalize for the number of digits $d$ in the numerator and denominator, there seems to be ambiguity about what exactly a trivial example is. With $d = 3$, $\frac{110}{220} = \frac{11}{22}$ is certainly a trivial example, and $\frac{101}{202} = \frac{11}{22}$ seems to be non-trivial. Then what about $\frac{100}{200} = \frac{10}{20}$, where you cancel out the middle zeroes? Should it still  count as a trivial example, since the resulting numerator and denominator is simply divided by $10$? In my solution, I have chosen to assert that $\frac{100}{200} = \frac{10}{20}$ is also non-trivial, if you cancel out the middle digit and not the last.

Nothing to say about the algorithm itself.
