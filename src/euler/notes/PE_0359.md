# [PE_0359](../PE_0359.java)
 
Let the last person in floor $1$ be $m$ (initially person $1$). Any following person $n$ in floor $1$ will follow two restrictions:
1) $n > m$
2) $m + n = x^2$

Looking at the sequence of triangle numbers $T(n) = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$, when adding consecutive terms we get $T(n) + T(n+1) = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} + \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{2} = (n+1)^2$ - a square.  
Since the first floor starts with $1$, $P(1, 2) = T(2) = 3$. Clearly, when $3 + n = x^2$ and $n > 3$, then $x^2$ can't be the same square as before. So we can write triangle numbers indefinitely to always get a square as the sum of two consecutive numbers, meaning the first floor is:
$$1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, \ldots$$

With some trial and error, here are the formulas for the general case of $P(f, r)$:
$$P(f, r) = \frac{f^2}{2} + (\frac{r}{2})^2 + 2((f-1)(\frac{r-1}{2}) + T(\frac{r-1}{2}), f > 1, f \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$$
$$P(f, r) = P(f+1, r) + f, f \equiv 0 \pmod{2}, r \equiv 0 \pmod{2}$$
$$P(f, r) = P(f+1, r) - f, f \equiv 0 \pmod{2}, r \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$$

Disappointingly, I cannot even begin to conceive the mathematical justification for these formulas, but somehow they work.
